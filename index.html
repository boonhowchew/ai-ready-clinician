Key concepts: AI, ML, Agents, API & MCP

Before exploring how AI is used in clinical practice, it helps to understand the language. Terms like machine learning, AI agents, API, and MCP are increasingly used in healthcare technology discussions — and each means something distinct. The infographic below offers a concise, non-technical overview of these five concepts, designed specifically for clinicians and healthcare professionals.

AI, ML, Agents, API & MCP: What Clinicians Need to Know

A quick guide to key concepts shaping modern healthcare technology

1
AI
The broad field
🧠
Artificial intelligence refers to computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — understanding language, recognising patterns, making predictions, or supporting decisions. AI is not a single technology but a broad field encompassing many approaches, from simple rule-based systems to sophisticated models that learn from data.
In healthcare

Clinical decision support that flags deteriorating patients, imaging tools that assist radiologists, AI scribes that draft clinical notes, and triage systems that prioritise urgency — all fall under the AI umbrella. Most clinicians encounter some form of AI daily, often embedded invisibly within the systems and workflows already in use around them.

2
ML
How many AI tools learn
📈
Machine learning is a branch of AI where models learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed for every rule. The model improves with exposure to more examples. Crucially, an ML model is only as reliable as the data it was trained on — making data quality and representativeness fundamental.
In healthcare

Predicting readmission risk, detecting early sepsis, classifying pathology slides, and flagging abnormalities on chest X-rays — these tools work by recognising patterns in large volumes of historical clinical data. The quality and representativeness of that training data directly determines whether the model performs reliably for the patients in your specific care setting.

3
AI Agents
AI that can act through steps
🤖
AI agents can understand a goal, plan multiple steps, use tools, retrieve information, and produce outputs — completing workflows rather than just answering questions. Unlike a chatbot responding to a single prompt, an agent initiates a sequence of actions to accomplish a more complex task with minimal human intervention.
In healthcare

An AI agent could draft a referral letter, check it against current guidelines, confirm the patient's latest results, and flag contraindications — all as steps in a single coordinated workflow. The clinician remains responsible for reviewing and approving every output the agent produces before it influences any clinical decision.

4
API
How any two systems communicate
🔌
An API (Application Programming Interface) is a universal set of rules that lets one software system request data or actions from another — securely, with defined permissions. APIs are not specific to AI; they underpin all modern connected software and have been the backbone of digital health infrastructure for decades.
In healthcare

When your EMR retrieves a lab result or a scheduling system checks bed availability, an API handles the exchange. AI tools use APIs — but so does every other piece of connected hospital software. Each API connection must be individually authorised, scoped to its intended purpose, and subject to regular audit.

5
MCP
Purpose-built for AI tools
🔗
MCP (Model Context Protocol) is a newer standard designed exclusively for AI applications — not general software. It defines how an AI model retrieves the external tools, data, and context it needs mid-task, while maintaining continuity across the interaction. Think of it as an API purpose-built for how AI actually works.
In healthcare

When a clinical AI assistant checks drug guidelines or retrieves a patient's allergy list during a consultation, MCP provides the standardised, auditable pathway — built for AI, not retrofitted from general software infrastructure. As AI tools become more capable, MCP-style standards will become essential for transparent and governable clinical deployment.

These five concepts often work together. In practice, a hospital might deploy an ML model (trained on clinical data) as part of an AI agent that uses an API to query the electronic health record — the same way any hospital software does — while MCP provides a standardised protocol specifically for that AI agent to access its tools and context mid-task. All of this operates under the broader umbrella of AI in healthcare. The key distinction worth remembering: APIs connect any software systems; MCP connects AI tools to the resources they need in a way designed for how AI actually works. Understanding each layer helps clinicians ask better questions, engage meaningfully with their technology teams, and exercise appropriate oversight when these tools touch patient care.

Session 1 · ~15 min read / 60–90 min facilitated

AI is Already Here. Where Are You?

Reduce anxiety, build honest self-awareness, and understand where AI is already operating in clinical practice — before anyone opens a device.

Awareness Self-assessment No devices needed

What AI is doing in clinical practice today

  • Clinical decision support — AI flagging drug interactions, sepsis risk scores, and deterioration alerts. Often already embedded in your EMR, invisibly. A 2026 Harvard-led study in Science found that a reasoning AI model matched or surpassed board-certified physicians at every stage of ER diagnostic reasoning — triage, initial evaluation, and admission decision-making.
  • Medical imaging — AI-assisted reads for chest X-rays, mammograms, and retinal scans. Some tools match specialist accuracy in narrow tasks.
  • Documentation & administration — AI scribes that listen to consultations and draft clinical notes; tools that summarise discharge letters and referrals.
  • Patient-facing tools — Symptom checkers, triage chatbots, medication reminders. Your patients are using these before they walk into your clinic.
  • Knowledge & research — Tools that summarise guidelines, surface relevant evidence, and generate differential diagnoses. A junior colleague who has read everything — but needs supervision.
What the evidence now shows

On AI agents in healthcare: A 2026 scoping review in PLOS ONE (Al-Ajlouni, 2026) mapped 43 studies on AI agents — conversational, workflow automation, and multimodal decision support systems. Nearly all were evaluated in simulated or laboratory settings. Safety data and real-world clinical outcomes were rarely reported. The gap between benchmark performance and deployment-readiness remains significant — and clinicians have a critical role in closing it. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0342182 ↗

What AI cannot replace — and what clinicians must become better at

As AI becomes stronger at pattern recognition, differential diagnosis, image interpretation, summarisation, and management suggestions, the clinician's human role becomes more visible — not less important. If AI becomes a better diagnostician for some defined tasks, doctors and healthcare professionals must become better at doctoring: listening, explaining, examining, comforting, contextualising, advocating, deciding under uncertainty, and carrying responsibility.

Reframing the question
The future clinician should not try to compete with AI as a faster database. The future clinician must use AI wisely while offering what patients most need from a human professional: understanding, judgement, compassion, trust, and accountability.
1. Listening and eliciting the patient's story
AI can process what is entered, but it does not sit with the patient, notice hesitation, ask with tenderness, or uncover what the patient fears, hides, forgets, or does not know is relevant.
2. Physical presence, examination, and clinical noticing
AI may analyse text, images, signals, and records, but the clinician still observes distress, frailty, pain, confusion, family dynamics, non-verbal cues, and examination findings in real time.
3. Explanation, trust, and shared decision-making
Patients need more than an answer. They need meaning, options, consequences, reassurance where appropriate, honesty where necessary, and decisions that fit their values, culture, resources, and goals of care.
4. Moral judgement, advocacy, and accountability
AI can suggest, rank, draft, and warn. It cannot bear professional responsibility, defend a vulnerable patient, navigate ethical conflict, or be accountable for what should be done for this patient at this time.
  • Clinical judgement: deciding whether the AI output fits the patient in front of you.
  • Human communication: translating diagnosis and uncertainty into language patients can understand and trust.
  • Contextual wisdom: recognising social constraints, patient preferences, local resources, and what the data does not capture.
  • Professional accountability: verifying, documenting, escalating, and taking responsibility for decisions made with AI support.
A practical rule for AI-ready clinicians
If AI becomes better at diagnosis, clinicians must become better at human care. Use AI to support thinking and workflow, but never allow it to replace the human work of listening, examining, explaining, contextualising, advocating, and taking responsibility.
Key message
The question isn't whether AI will change your practice. It already is. The question is whether you're in the conversation — or whether that conversation happens without you.

Let's Actually Use It

Hands-on experience with real AI tools — safely, using fictional patient cases — to build practical confidence and critical thinking.

The one rule that cannot be broken
Never enter real patient data — names, IC numbers, dates of birth, diagnoses, clinical findings — into any public AI tool. This includes ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Today we use fictional patients only.

Tools we use today

Claude · claude.ai
Strong at nuanced reasoning and long documents. Tends to be transparent about uncertainty — useful in clinical contexts.
ChatGPT · chatgpt.com
The most widely known tool. Fast and capable. Familiar to most who have tried AI before.
Gemini · gemini.google.com
Google's model. Useful for search-integrated tasks. May feel familiar to Google Workspace users.
The wider LLM landscape
These three tools are used for the workshop because they are familiar and easy to access. They are not the only LLMs. Other examples include: United States/global tools — Microsoft Copilot, Perplexity, Grok, and Meta AI; China-based tools — DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, ERNIE Bot / Wenxin Yiyan, Doubao, and GLM / Zhipu; and Malaysia-linked models — ILMU by YTL AI Labs, MaLLaM by Mesolitica, and Malaysian Mistral by Mesolitica. Session 3 · ~20 min read / 75 min facilitated

AI, Ethics & My Responsibility

Move from using AI to owning your role in it — through case-based discussion, a practical ethics framework, and honest conversation about accountability.

Ethics Case scenarios Administrators included

Where AI goes wrong in clinical contexts

  • Hallucination — AI generating plausible but factually incorrect outputs with full confidence. Wrong drug doses, fabricated references, invented guidelines.
  • Training data bias — Models trained on certain populations performing worse for others. Often invisible until an audit reveals a pattern.
  • Blind trust / Automation bias — The human tendency to over-trust automated outputs and stop thinking critically. The most dangerous failure because it doesn't look like one.
  • Data privacy failure — Patient data in unapproved tools, stored on overseas servers, used for commercial model training. Often through well-intentioned efficiency-seeking.
  • Accountability gaps — When AI-assisted decisions cause harm, legal and ethical responsibility is frequently unclear. Frameworks are still catching up.
  • Dehumanisation — When AI mediates triage, intake, or diagnosis, it can reduce patients to data points and clinicians to passive intermediaries. The therapeutic relationship — empathy, context, the doctor–patient dyad — is eroded by design, not by accident. Shared decision-making becomes a triad where the patient's story may never fully arrive.
  • Misaligned optimisation — AI systems are built to optimise a defined target. When that target is throughput, bed occupancy, or cost reduction rather than patient outcome, the tool can perform exactly as designed and still cause harm. The failure is not a bug — it is what was asked for.
  • Overconfident forecasting — AI outputs can project an 82% sepsis risk with the same visual authority as a blood result. When irreducible clinical uncertainty is hidden behind numerical precision, clinicians may act on a certainty the model cannot actually support — and patients may receive a prognosis that was never real.

Framework partially informed by: Müller H, Patel VL, Shortliffe EH, Jurisica I & Holzinger A. "Seven deadly sins in artificial intelligence for digital medicine." npj Digital Medicine (2026). doi.org/10.1038/s41746-026-02607-4 ↗

Case scenarios for discussion

Tap each scenario to expand. Discuss in small groups — 8–9 minutes each. There are no definitively correct answers.

Scenario A — The confident AI Clinical ▼

A GP uses an AI decision support tool to review a complex patient's medications. Running behind schedule, she accepts the tool's recommendation to stop metformin — without checking that morning's eGFR, which showed an improvement. The patient's glucose becomes poorly controlled for three weeks.

Discuss: Who bears responsibility — the GP, the hospital, or the vendor? · What workflow safeguard could have prevented this? · Does time pressure change the ethical calculus?

Facilitator insight▼
On responsibility

Responsibility is shared but not equally distributed. The GP bears primary professional accountability — accepting a tool's recommendation without independent verification is not the same as deferring to a specialist opinion. The tool use does not transfer liability; it may compound it if the clinician cannot demonstrate that clinical judgement was exercised. The hospital bears responsibility for the workflow design that allowed a high-stakes decision to be made under time pressure without a verification step. The vendor bears responsibility for how prominently the tool presents uncertainty and what safeguards are built into the interface.

On automation bias

The real failure here is not the AI — it is automation bias. The GP did not stop thinking critically because she lacked intelligence; she stopped because the output was plausible, authoritative in appearance, and arrived at a moment of cognitive depletion. This is predictable and designable-around. A workflow safeguard as simple as a confirmation prompt — "This recommendation involves stopping an active medication. Have you reviewed today's labs?" — could have prevented the outcome. The lesson is not to distrust AI tools, but to design clinical workflows that preserve the moment of independent judgement even under pressure.

On time pressure

Time pressure does not change the ethical calculus — but it does reveal a system design problem. A GP who is regularly too busy to verify high-stakes AI outputs is a GP working in an unsafe system. The ethical obligation is not merely individual; it includes raising the systemic conditions that make lapses predictable.

Scenario B — The efficiency temptation Privacy ▼

An administrator pastes a 'de-identified' outcome dataset into ChatGPT to help draft a quality report — no names, but diagnoses, dates, and ward numbers intact. A colleague later points out that the combination is enough to re-identify patients in a small hospital.

Discuss: Was this a data breach? · What does 'de-identified' actually mean in practice? · What institutional policy would prevent this?

Facilitator insight▼
On whether this was a data breach

Almost certainly yes, under most data protection frameworks including Malaysia's PDPA and GDPR-equivalent legislation. The legal test for de-identification is not whether names are absent — it is whether a reasonable effort could re-identify individuals from the remaining data. Diagnoses, admission dates, and ward numbers in a small hospital constitute a combination of quasi-identifiers sufficient for re-identification. The administrator's intent — efficient drafting — is irrelevant to whether a breach occurred. Good intent does not create safe data.

On what de-identified actually means

De-identification is not a binary state — it is a spectrum determined by context. Data that is genuinely anonymous in a large national dataset may be re-identifiable in a 200-bed district hospital. The standard requires not just removing direct identifiers but assessing the re-identification risk of the remaining fields in the specific deployment context. This is a technical and legal judgement, not something a non-specialist administrator can reliably perform by removing a name column.

On institutional policy

The most effective policy is a clearly maintained approved tools list — a short, visible document specifying which AI tools are sanctioned for which data types, with explicit prohibitions on pasting patient-adjacent data into consumer AI products. Paired with a brief mandatory training module, this makes the right choice easy and the wrong choice conscious. The administrator in this scenario was likely not aware they were doing something impermissible; the policy failure preceded the individual error.

Scenario C — The invisible disparity Equity ▼

A hospital deploys an AI triage tool trained on a different demographic. Over six months, staff notice certain ethnic groups consistently receive lower urgency scores than their presentation warrants — but no single incident is serious enough to escalate. An audit a year later confirms a statistically significant disparity in wait times.

Discuss: At what point did this become an ethical failure — deployment, first observation, or the audit? · What is each staff member's individual duty? · What governance structure should have caught this earlier?

Facilitator insight▼
On when the ethical failure began

The first ethical failure was at deployment. A tool trained on a demographic materially different from the hospital's patient population should not have been deployed without pre-deployment fairness testing — including subgroup performance analysis by ethnicity, age, and clinical presentation type. The absence of that testing, or the decision to deploy despite known uncertainty, is where accountability begins. The first observation by staff was a second failure — the point at which harm became a known risk. The audit a year later was a third failure: confirmation of what was observable much earlier. Waiting for statistical significance before acting is not a defensible ethical standard when individual staff had already noticed a pattern.

On each staff member's individual duty

Every staff member who noticed the pattern had a professional obligation to say something — not to conduct an audit themselves, but to report the observation through whatever channel exists (clinical lead, patient safety officer, quality team). The absence of a serious incident does not extinguish the duty to report a concern. If no reporting channel existed, that itself is information that belongs with the hospital's governance team. The nurse in the Session 3 quiz question was not failing ethically by misjudging the severity — they were being failed by a system with no escalation pathway for soft signals.

On governance

Effective governance of deployed AI tools requires prospective monitoring commitments, not just reactive audits. At deployment, the hospital should have established: what subgroup performance metrics will be monitored, at what frequency, and what the threshold is for triggering a review. This is standard practice for any medical device post-market surveillance and should apply equivalently to AI clinical tools. The structure that should have caught this earlier is a scheduled, mandatory performance review at 3 and 6 months post-deployment — with subgroup breakdowns reported to a clinical AI oversight committee.

Scenario D — The patient who came prepared Communication & Conduct ▼

A 58-year-old woman with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes arrives having already consulted an AI symptom checker and spent an evening with ChatGPT. She hands you a printed summary recommending she start insulin immediately, highlighted and annotated in her own handwriting. Your clinical decision support system suggests metformin as first-line. She looks at you directly and asks: "Which one should I trust — the AI or you?" She is not confused or hostile. She is engaged, informed on her own terms, and genuinely wants to understand.

Part 1 — The conversation in the room (5–6 min)

How do you explain the difference between a validated clinical tool and a consumer AI product — in her language, without dismissing the effort she put in? · What would you say about uncertainty, including the fact that even your own tool is a recommendation, not a verdict? · How do you preserve her trust and sense of agency while steering toward the evidence-based plan? · What part of this conversation could no AI on either side of the desk have done for you?

Facilitator insight — Part 1▼
On explaining the difference without dismissing her

Avoid "the AI you used is unreliable" — it dismisses her effort and creates a binary she doesn't need. A more honest and respectful framing: "The tool you used is trained on a very large general population — it's impressive at spotting patterns across millions of people, but it doesn't know you specifically. What I use is validated on patients who've been seen in systems like ours and reviewed against clinical guidelines. Both are tools — mine is calibrated more carefully for this decision." This positions her research as legitimate while explaining why calibration matters.

On communicating uncertainty

Uncertainty is not a weakness — it is honest medicine. A useful frame: "My tool suggests metformin as the most common starting point for someone with your profile. It doesn't know your preferences, your lifestyle, or what trade-offs matter most to you. That's the part I need to understand before we decide together." This keeps the AI output as one input, names the limit of what it can offer, and immediately moves into the relational space no tool occupies.

On what only the clinician can do

The patient didn't come annotating printouts because she wanted to be corrected. She came because she is engaged, slightly anxious, and trying to take control of a frightening diagnosis. No AI — on either side — captured that. The conversation about what insulin or tablets mean to her daily life, what she's afraid of, what "manageable" looks like for her — that is the irreplaceable part. The tool answers a population question; you answer a personal one.

Part 2 — After she leaves (3–4 min)

The patient conversation is only half the professional picture. Consider three distinct responsibilities that follow from this encounter.

Your clinical note

Does it reflect that you exercised independent judgement — not that you deferred to a tool, and not that you overrode one without reason? If this record were reviewed tomorrow, could it account for your decision?

Your colleague

You refer her to a dietitian who will also use a digital care platform. Do they know an AI tool was part of today's encounter — and that this patient arrives with her own AI-generated information? Does that context change what the referral letter needs to say?

Your institution

The consumer AI tool she used before arriving is increasingly common among your patients. Is there a place in your team or governance structure where that observation belongs — or does it disappear because no single case caused serious harm?

Facilitator insight — Part 2▼
Your clinical note

A note that reads only "metformin commenced" is insufficient in an AI-augmented encounter. Good documentation here captures: what clinical decision support was used and what it suggested; what additional factors informed your independent judgement (her lifestyle, renal function, preferences); that the patient presented with AI-generated information and how it was addressed; and the shared decision-making process. This is not administrative burden — it is the record that protects both patient and clinician if the decision is ever questioned.

Your colleague

The dietitian will likely encounter the same printed summary. A referral letter that notes "patient has been actively self-researching using consumer AI tools and is well-engaged — discussed the distinction between validated clinical tools and general AI products" gives the next clinician the context to continue the conversation, not start it from scratch. Continuity of care in the AI era includes continuity of the AI literacy conversation. This patient's engagement is a clinical feature worth passing on.

Your institution

No single encounter with a patient's AI printout is a governance emergency. But it is a signal. Clinicians are the best early-warning system for how AI is reaching their patient population — and most of that intelligence is never surfaced because there is no mechanism for it. A brief note to a digital health lead, a mention at a departmental meeting, or a flag through a patient safety near-miss system are all proportionate responses. The pattern matters more than any single case, and patterns only become visible when clinicians report what they observe.

Professional conduct in the AI era is a habit of thinking one step beyond the tool and one step beyond the room.

The 5 questions to ask before acting on AI

Tap each question to mark it as reviewed. Use this as a thinking habit, not a compliance checklist.

  • 1. Where did this output come from?Do I know what data this model was trained on? Is it validated for patients like mine — in terms of age, ethnicity, geography, and condition?
  • 2. Has this tool been approved for this use?Is this a consumer product or an institutionally validated clinical tool? Has my hospital's governance team reviewed it?
  • 3. Am I thinking critically or just accepting?Am I genuinely evaluating this output, or have I already decided to trust it because it sounds authoritative? Would I check this if a junior colleague said it?
  • 4. Could this output harm a specific patient group?Is there reason to think this recommendation might be less reliable for this particular patient — based on demographics or circumstances the model may not have seen?
  • 5. If this goes wrong, can I account for my decision?Can I explain clearly why I acted on this output? Does my documentation reflect that I exercised clinical judgement — not just deferred to a tool?

What a thoughtful answer includes▼
Good answers avoid technical shorthand — "the algorithm flagged" or "the system said" — because they position AI as an authority the patient cannot interrogate. More useful language: "I used a tool that looks at patients with similar health profiles and highlights patterns I should consider" or "There's a programme that helps me check whether I've missed anything." The goal is transparency without jargon — the patient deserves to know AI was involved in their care, and to feel that you are still the one making the decision. If you find yourself unable to explain it simply, that is a signal worth reflecting on.
2 What would you want them to understand about what the AI can and cannot do?
What a thoughtful answer includes▼
What AI can do: process large amounts of data consistently, surface patterns across populations, reduce certain types of human error, and flag things that might otherwise be missed in a busy clinical day.

What AI cannot do: know this patient's values, fears, life circumstances, or what they haven't told you yet. It cannot understand what trade-offs matter to them. It cannot be responsible for the decision — and patients should know that responsibility stays with a person, not a system.

A patient who understands this distinction is better equipped to engage with AI in their own healthcare — not just in this encounter, but in every interaction with symptom checkers, health apps, and AI tools they will encounter independently.
3 What part of that conversation could no AI replace?
What a thoughtful answer includes▼
Patients do not consent to treatment — they consent to a clinician they trust. That trust is built on relationship, continuity, and the sense that someone who knows them is making a judgement on their behalf. The part no AI replaces is the moral weight of the decision: someone has to own it, and that ownership is what the therapeutic relationship is built on.

Good answers often point to something specific: the patient who finally admitted they were scared. The family member in the corner of the room. The moment when the plan changed because of something said at the end of the appointment that no algorithm would have surfaced. That specificity is the point — it is not abstract human value, it is the particular human moment that happened in your clinic, with your patient, because you were there.

Your answer to that last question is the most important thing you will take from this programme. It is what keeps the patient at the centre — even as the tools change around them.

Professional Standards AI in Clinical Practice: Core Professional Obligations

Across medical regulatory bodies internationally, four core obligations recur consistently when physicians use AI in clinical practice. These principles underpin professional codes of conduct whether formally codified in national guidelines or embedded in general standards of medical ethics. They apply regardless of the setting, the tool, or the clinical context.

1
Informed Consent

Patients must be told that an AI tool is being used in their care and must understand its benefits, limitations, and risks before a clinical decision is made using it. Consent is not implied by the patient attending the consultation — it must be actively obtained and documented. This obligation applies both to institutionally validated tools and to any AI product a clinician chooses to use in the clinical encounter.

2
Retained Clinical Accountability

AI involvement does not transfer, dilute, or share the physician's professional accountability for a clinical decision. Regardless of what a tool recommends, the clinician remains responsible. Accepting an AI output without independent clinical judgement is not a defence — it is the failure. Professional codes of conduct require that practitioners evaluate AI recommendations through their own clinical insight, not simply act on them.

3
Disclosure in Medical Records

AI use in clinical decision-making must be disclosed in the patient's medical record. The documentation should reflect that AI contributed to the encounter and how it informed the clinician's reasoning — not merely record the outcome. A note that reads only "metformin commenced" when a clinical AI tool and a patient's own AI research both featured in the decision is an incomplete record under any robust professional standard.

4
Adequate Training Before Clinical Use

Practitioners are expected to develop the competencies required to apply AI critically before using it in patient care. This means understanding how the tool works, what it was trained on, where it may fail, and what its outputs mean in clinical context. Deploying an AI tool without that understanding is not expedient — it is a breach of established professional standards. This programme is one starting point; the obligation to maintain competency is ongoing.

For Malaysian medical practitioners: these obligations are formally codified and enforceable under the Malaysian Medical Council (MMC) Guideline on the Ethical Use of AI in Medical Practice (Version 1/2025), which is grounded in the MMC's Code of Professional Conduct. The full guideline is available at mmc.gov.my ↗

The facilitator

My Story

How a family medicine specialist found his way into AI — and why he built this programme.

"I want to start not with a slide, and not with a definition of AI. I want to start with a confession.

A while back, I watched something — a documentary, a video — and something shifted. I don't think I can fully explain why it hit me the way it did. But I remember sitting there thinking: this isn't just the future. This is already happening. And it is going to change how I see patients, do clinical research, how I make decisions, how I teach and practise medicine.

Now, I should tell you — I wasn't anxious about that. I was curious. Maybe even excited. Which I realise puts me in a minority in this part of my world and fields.

But here's what I want you to know. That curiosity didn't immediately translate into competence. It took months. Months of reading things I half understood. Trying tools that sometimes impressed me and sometimes frustrated me. Going down rabbit holes that led nowhere useful. There was no single course that handed me a certificate and told me I was AI-ready. It was gradual, a little messy, and very much self-directed.

What kept me going was one question I kept coming back to, sitting in my clinic and my university's office: could this help me take better care of my patients, and do better research? Not replace my judgement. Not do my job for me. But help me think better, notice more, decide more carefully. That question made it feel worth the effort.

I'm standing here today because I think many of you have never been given the chance to ask that question in a safe space. You've been too busy. Or the topic feels too technical, too far from your world. Or honestly — and I say this with complete respect — it might feel a little threatening to your identity as a clinician, a specialist or subspecialist who has spent years building expertise.

I get that. What I want to offer today is not a masterclass. It's just an honest conversation about where we are, what AI is actually doing in clinical practice and scientific research right now, and how you might begin — at your own pace, without shame, and without having to spend a single cent on an expensive course.

You don't need to become an AI expert. You just need to stop feeling like it's happening to you, and start feeling like you have some say in it. That's what today is about."
